Breakpoint classification framework

Two-layer classification for cross-species breakpoints (click to toggle)

Cross-species breakpoints (page 13) and inversion candidates (pages 1, 5) are classified along two independent axes. The architecture class (Layer 1) describes what kind of breakpoint it is. The age model (Layer 2) describes when the rearrangement most likely happened. Both axes use the evidence already in the atlas (TE flanking density, local PCA bands, synteny block edges, dXY) plus future layers (10-species synteny, miniprot anchors) when they ship.

Layer 1 — Architecture

A Simple inversion Two clean inversion edges. Local PCA / MDS bands support inversion. No strong synteny boundary with related species. Ordinary polymorphic inversion.
B Synteny-boundary inversion Inversion edge overlaps the edge of a conserved syntenic block. Same block boundary exists in related species, but chromosome identity is mostly conserved. The inversion may reuse a pre-existing genomic boundary.
C Fusion/fission-associated One inversion breakpoint overlaps a cross-species chromosome split/join point. One species has the region on one chromosome; another species has homologous material split across two chromosomes. The inversion may be anchored at an older karyotype breakpoint.
D Terminal translocation / reversed attachment Region is near a chromosome end. Homologous segment appears on a different chromosome in another species, possibly in reversed orientation. Synteny suggests terminal segment movement. A chromosome-end segment may have moved or reattached in another genomic context.
E Recurrent rearrangement hotspot The same region carries inversion AND translocation/fusion/fission AND orientation-switch signals — plus repeat-rich / low-mappability / telomeric / centromeric features. Multiple species may show different rearrangements around the same point. This region may repeatedly break and repair in different ways. Scientific label: recurrent breakage-and-repair hotspot.
F Ambiguous / assembly-risk Gap near breakpoint, low mappability, collapsed repeat, scaffold join, weak SV support, only one breakpoint visible. Do not over-interpret yet.

Layer 2 — Age model

YOUNG-POP Inversion only seen inside the focal population. Low dXY between standard / inverted haplotypes. Long shared haplotype around breakpoint. No evidence in related species. The inversion is likely recent.
OLD-POLY Strong dXY elevation inside the inversion. Deep standard-vs-inverted haplotype split. Arrangement-specific phylogeny. Possibly shared across populations / species. The inversion itself may be old.
OLD-BP / YOUNG-INV Inversion is polymorphic only now / recently, but its breakpoint overlaps an ancient synteny / karyotype / repeat boundary. dXY inside the inversion is not extremely old. The fragile region is old, but the inversion using it may be younger.
LINEAGE-KARYO Breakpoint explains a difference between species' chromosomes. Outgroup suggests the event happened on one lineage. Fusion / fission / translocation is fixed between species. Mostly a species-evolution breakpoint, not a population inversion.
MULTI-AGE-HOTSPOT Breakpoint substrate appears ancient. Karyotype change happened at one time. Present-day inversion / translocation polymorphism may be younger. Same region shows several rearrangement types. Different rearrangement events of different ages reused the same fragile region.

Combined label format

candidate_id | architecture | age_model | confidence

Example: LG28_candidate_X | Class D/E (terminal translocation + recurrent hotspot) | OLD-BP/YOUNG-INV | medium

Operational structural-haplotype framework [v4 turn 87]

Local PCA bands are observed clusters, not biological genotypes. The mapping from band to genotype requires per-sample heterozygosity / haplotype-divergence evidence that local PCA alone cannot supply. The two display vocabularies (legacy / detailed, see Vocabulary table above) reflect this distinction: legacy labels make no claim; detailed labels offer an operational ordering by median PC1.

Why a single inversion gives 3 bands. A simple biallelic inversion has two haploid arrangements (call them H1 and H2). At the diploid individual level these produce three unordered classes: H1/H1, H1/H2, H2/H2 — three local-PCA bands. The traditional "REF / HET / INV" naming is replaced here by H1/H1, H1/H2, H2/H2 because local PCA does not directly prove which haplotype is reference-like or inverted; the H-system avoids that unsupported claim.

Why some candidates show 4–6 bands. A region may contain more than two major structural haplotypes. Three haplotypes (H1, H2, H3) produce up to six diploid combinations: H1/H1, H1/H2, H2/H2, H1/H3, H2/H3, H3/H3. Six bands does not require six orientations; it can mean three underlying haplotype classes producing six diploid combinations. Intermediate cases (4 or 5 visible bands) occur when one or more diploid combinations is rare, missing, or merged in the PCA projection.

How heterozygosity disambiguates. Local PCA alone cannot tell whether a band is H1/H1, H1/H2, etc. The disambiguating evidence is per-sample heterozygosity: homozygote-like classes should show low within-sample haplotype divergence, heterozygote-like classes should show elevated divergence. A band may also contain two hidden heterozygosity modes (e.g., 50% low-het + 50% high-het in the same PC band) — these need to be split into sub-bands. Per-sample heterozygosity is part of pages 2 (θπ) and is being added; until it ships, the detailed labels are operational only.

Manuscript-safe wording. When writing about candidates, prefer:

  • "candidate regions were classified into local structural-haplotype bands; bands were further interpreted as homozygote-like, heterozygote-like, mixed, or ambiguous based on within-sample haplotype divergence" — instead of "candidate inversions were classified as REF/REF, REF/INV, and INV/INV"
  • "six stable bands are consistent with a multi-haplotype structural system, potentially involving three major haplotype backgrounds and their diploid combinations" — instead of "six bands indicate multiple inversions"
  • "this band is H1/H1-like under an operational labeling scheme" — instead of "this band is AA"
  • "reference-like" or "inversion-like" only if independently supported by orientation / breakpoint / long-read evidence — never as a default

Caveats per band-count interpretation.

3 bandsLikely simple biallelic system: H1/H1, H1/H2, H2/H2.
4 bandsCould be a biallelic system plus one split class, one missing/merged class from a 3-haplotype system, asymmetry due to sample frequency, or one hidden axis not fully separated.
5 bandsCould be 3 homozygote-like + 2 heterozygote-like classes, with one expected heterozygote class missing, rare, selected against, or merged.
6 bandsCould be the full 3-haplotype diploid system: H1/H1, H1/H2, H2/H2, H1/H3, H2/H3, H3/H3.
No bandsNo strong local structural haplotype signal, too little variation, or insufficient local contrast.

Band order in PC1×PC2 space need not follow a biologically clean sequence. PCA is a projection — observed band order can be (e.g.) H1/H1, H1/H2, H2/H2, H1/H3 even though biologically H2/H3 might be expected to fall between H2/H2 and H3/H3. The detailed labels assigned by the resolver use the most parsimonious sub-pattern from the 3-haplotype model, ordered by median PC1; sub-pattern correctness has to be confirmed against heterozygosity evidence.

Manuscript vocabulary [v4 turn 100]

We use a tiered vocabulary that separates what is observed from what is inferred. Local PCA produces stable banding patterns at certain genomic positions — that is the observation. The inference is that those banding patterns reflect segregating haplotype classes. The further inference that those classes correspond to inversion orientations requires independent evidence and is reserved for the strongest cases.

structural-haplotype system Default term — a region where individuals segregate into stable local haplotype classes across adjacent windows. Conservative; makes no orientation claim. Use this whenever stable band assignments are observed but breakpoint or orientation evidence is missing.
segregating structural-haplotype system Long form, used when emphasizing that the system is polymorphic (multiple haplotype classes coexisting in the cohort), as opposed to a fixed difference between species or populations.
inversion-like structural-haplotype system Stronger term — used when the system shows signatures consistent with an inversion (long-range LD block, recombination suppression, distinct PCA bands across many windows) but the breakpoints are not yet localized.
candidate polymorphic inversion Reserved for systems with breakpoint or orientation evidence (DELLY/Manta calls, long-read split reads, synteny breaks, etc.). Use only when the inversion claim is independently supported.
polymorphic inversion The strongest term — only when orientation is confirmed (e.g., long-read assembly of both arrangements, validated breakpoints). Almost never used in this manuscript without the "candidate" qualifier.

Single-paragraph methods statement (paste-ready):

"We first identified segregating structural-haplotype systems from stable local-PCA banding patterns. A subset of these systems showed inversion-like signatures and were further classified as candidate polymorphic inversions when independent breakpoint or orientation evidence was available. The term structural-haplotype system is used throughout as a conservative descriptor for regions with stable local haplotype segregation; the term inversion is reserved for candidates with supporting orientation or breakpoint evidence."

L1/L2/L3 hierarchy in the new vocabulary

The atlas's existing L1/L2/L3 envelope hierarchy maps cleanly onto the structural-haplotype framework:

L1 Broad segregating structural-haplotype region — a coarse chromosomal block where local PCA shows banded structure. May contain one or more L2 sub-blocks. The L1 envelope is the outer container; it does not yet imply a specific haplotype-class count or any inversion claim.
L2 Stable internal haplotype block — a sub-region of an L1 envelope where the K-means band assignments are stable (consistent across adjacent windows, low boundary signal). Each L2 has its own K, σ-verdict, and (in detailed mode) per-band heterozygosity profile. Multiple L2s within one L1 may share haplotype classes (in which case they merge under L1-merged catalogue view) or carry distinct ones.
L3 Refined candidate structural-haplotype interval — a further sub-block within an L2 where additional internal substructure is resolved (e.g., multimodal heterozygosity inside a band, sub-band splitting). L3 is the level at which detailed-mode candidates can have biologically distinct sub-haplotypes within a single PCA band.

The atlas's column "Regimes" annotates user-curated structural-haplotype systems, where one regime corresponds to one segregating structural-haplotype system that may span multiple L2 envelopes. The L1/L2/L3 hierarchy is the geometric subdivision; the regime registry is the biological-system annotation on top of it.

Band-reach + regime breadth [v4 turn 103-104]

Band-reach is the per-fish count of distinct K-means bands visited across a contiguous region. Fish that stay in 1–2 bands across many adjacent L2 envelopes are "lane-disciplined" — they are stable carriers of a particular structural-haplotype background. Fish that visit many bands either carry recombinant chromosomes (different haplotype backgrounds in different windows) or the local PCA has lost regime structure in that region.

Regime breadth is the per-region summary of band-reach values across all fish:

narrow ≥70% of fish have band-reach ≤2 across the windowed region. The cohort segregates cleanly into stable lanes — strong segregating-structural-haplotype-system signal. Many fish stay in a single band across the L2 chain, with the cohort distributed among multiple bands.
medium 30–70% of fish have band-reach ≤2. Mixed regime — partial segregation with some lane-crossing fish.
wide <30% of fish have band-reach ≤2. Most fish visit many bands. No clean structural-haplotype segregation in this region. Could mean: high recombination, small effective sample sizes per band, or low local-PCA discrimination.
no_signal Fewer than 2 bands populated (≥5 fish each). The region does not have the band structure needed to call regime breadth.

The regime toggle on the per-sample lines panel paints a thin colored strip above the plot showing per-L2 regime breadth (green=narrow, amber=medium, red=wide, grey=no_signal). On LG28, narrow regions on the left transition to wide regions on the right — this is the visible banding-discipline pattern that distinguishes segregating-structural-haplotype regions from non-segregating regions.

Hatchery applications [v4 turn 105]

Structural-haplotype systems are genomic units for broodstock management. They are not breeding recommendations on their own, but they define the units that breeding programs can track, preserve, and test for trait association.

Use cases:

1. Hidden-diversity surveillance A hatchery may know that fish A is from family 1 and fish B is from family 2, but not know that only 8 fish in the entire broodstock carry haplotype H3 at candidate region X. If those 8 fish are removed, that linked block disappears and may not be quickly recreated by recombination. The atlas surfaces this hidden layer.
2. Region-specific inbreeding Two fish may look unrelated by genome-wide FROH but be H1/H1 at a candidate region containing fertility, growth, or immune genes. Mating them creates offspring with high local homozygosity. Region-specific inbreeding is invisible to overall pedigree-based metrics.
3. Marker-assisted broodstock management Once candidate systems are catalogued, hatcheries can design cheap region-specific markers (KASP, amplicon sequencing, small SNP panels) and screen routine broodstock without re-running local PCA. The atlas → marker-design → routine-screen pipeline is the most direct practical value.
4. Trait-association units Structural-haplotype classes are better association units than individual SNPs because each class represents a whole inherited block. Future trait studies (growth, survival, deformity, fertility, disease resistance, hybrid offspring performance) test H1/H1 vs H1/H2 vs H2/H2 directly rather than millions of correlated SNPs.
5. Mating-design support (after validation) Once associations are validated, a simple decision table can guide mating. e.g.: "avoid H1/H1 × H1/H1 if that combination has low survival; prefer H1/H1 × H2/H2 if heterozygote advantage is documented." Validation is required — the atlas provides candidates, not breeding rules.

Manuscript-safe wording for the discussion section:

"These structural-haplotype systems are not immediately breeding recommendations, but they define genomic units that can be tracked, preserved, and tested for association with aquaculture traits. They reveal a hidden layer of broodstock diversity: stable linked haplotype backgrounds that may not be captured by family labels or genome-wide diversity summaries alone. A subset of these systems can be developed into cheap region-specific markers for routine broodstock screening, providing a practical foundation for marker-assisted broodstock management."

Why these regions matter for diversity preservation:

"These regions represent stable segregating structural-haplotype diversity: linked genomic backgrounds that are not rapidly broken down by recombination and may therefore be lost as entire blocks if their carriers are removed from the breeding population."

ConcordFraction of samples assigned to the same K-means group in two adjacent L2s after Hungarian alignment. High = L2s are interchangeable; suggests merge.
Cramér's VEffect-size measure of association between two clusterings. N-independent. <0.10 small, 0.10–0.30 medium, >0.30 large.
p-valueSignificance of the contingency. With persistent sample structure, p ≈ 0 is expected and confirms association — read together with concord.
ConservationMean concord with left + right L2 neighbors. Low (orange) = the L2 boundary is biologically real.

Multi-evidence regime framework [v4.0 doctrine, t14e]
K-means bands are GEOMETRIC, not biological

Doctrine (per Quentin, t14e): K-means bands are provisional geometric clusters, not final biological regimes. Final regime calls require multi-evidence convergence. K=6 frequently represents nested substructure inside three major regimes — do NOT default to interpreting K=6 as six biological regimes. The paper-level call should usually use major regimes; higher K values are diagnostic / substructure layers.

Workflow:

  1. K-means proposes local PCA bands (K=3 default; K=6 for substructure check).
  2. Dosage + GHSL + heterozygosity + ROH/F_ROH + family/relatedness each independently validate or reject the biological interpretation.
  3. Candidate-level regime calls are assigned only when multiple evidence layers agree.

Per-band layer scope (refinement t14e+): not every layer informs every band equally. The convergence rule is scope-aware — a layer marked low_info for a given band does not push toward STRONG nor toward SUSPECT. See SCHEMA_V2.md §14.2.5 for the full scope table.

  • GHSL — informs ALL bands (every band has a haplotype signature).
  • Heterozygosity — primary scope is the HET band; homozygous bands have ~0% het by definition, so any apparent het differentiation in HOMOs is dosage noise, not signal.
  • θπ (per-band π) — informative for HET (mixing two haplotype families) and as a cross-band asymmetry probe (reduced π in INV-fixed bands).
  • F_ROH — primary scope is HOMO bands (high F_ROH there can mask the band identity as IBD).
  • Family — confounder check for all bands; family LD can produce any band.

UI plan — a single mode-switcher above the L3 panes (and on the karyotype page), with one button per evidence layer:

  • K-means — current behavior. PCA-derived geometric bands. Always available.
  • Dosage — per-band marker dosage pattern (mean dose by group, dose distribution). Validates geometric bands as REF/HET/INV-like.
  • GHSL — per-band haplotype-similarity-by-locus. Validates whether bands diverge in haplotype space.
  • Heterozygosity — per-band dosage heterozygosity. The HET band should sit between the two homozygous bands.
  • ROH / F_ROH — inbreeding / IBD confounder check. A "band" that is just a high-F_ROH cluster is not a regime.
  • Family / relatedness — kinship confounder check. A "band" that is family-pure (high fam_purity) is family LD, not a regime.

Convergence rule (provisional, lock after 226-sample LG28 calibration):

  • Strong regime = K-means + dosage + GHSL all consistent AND ROH + family layers do NOT explain the band geometry.
  • Suspect regime = K-means alone, with ≥1 confounder layer flagged.
  • Rejected band = ROH or family fully explains the apparent K-means band → it is NOT a biological regime.

R-side prerequisites (NOT YET WRITTEN — parallel chat needed):

  • STEP_R30_emit_per_band_dosage.R — for each L2 × K-band, mean / median / quartile of marker dose; emit to per_band_dosage JSON layer.
